Does Further Economic Growth Make Citizens of Already Rich Countries Happier? Arguments and Reasoning

The that once a country is already rich, additional economic wealth does not increase its citizens' happiness is one of the most debated questions in the social sciences. It traces back to what is known as the Easterlin Paradox - the observation, first proposed by Richard Easterlin in 1974, that average happiness levels in developed countries did not rise over time despite substantial economic growth [1], [2]. Below, I present the key arguments supporting this view, organized by their underlying reasoning.

1. The Hedonic Treadmill (Adaptation)

One of the most powerful arguments is the hedonic treadmill (also called adaptation theory). The core idea is that humans have a relatively stable "set point" of happiness, determined largely by genetics and personality. Positive or negative life events - including increases in income - may temporarily shift well-being above or below this set point, but people quickly adapt and return to their level [3]. As Diener, Lucas, and Scollon (2006) explain, the classic hedonic treadmill model implies that "good and bad events temporarily affect happiness, but people quickly adapt back to hedonic ," which would mean that "individual and societal efforts to increase happiness are doomed to failure" [3].

In the context of wealthy nations, this means that as the entire society grows richer, aspirations and expectations rise . What was once considered a luxury (e.g., a car, a smartphone, international travel) soon becomes a perceived necessity, and the incremental happiness boost from each new income increase diminishes. Easterlin himself argued that "an increase in income, and thus in the goods at one's disposal, does not bring with it a lasting increase in happiness because of the negative effect on utility of hedonic " [1]. People fail to anticipate the extent of this adaptation, which leads them to in income generation at the expense of other life domains.

The revised hedonic treadmill model acknowledges that set points can change under some circumstances, but still supports the idea that adaptation to material gains is and that income increases produce only happiness effects [3].

2. The Positional Treadmill (Social Comparison)

A second crucial argument concerns income and social comparison. Humans evaluate their well-being not in absolute terms, but relative to others - a phenomenon in evolutionary psychology and studied extensively in the literature. Clark, Frijters, and Shields (2008) provide a comprehensive review of how relative income shapes happiness: "Income may be evaluated relative to others (social comparison) or to oneself in the past " [4].

This creates a positional treadmill: when everyone in a country grows richer simultaneously, one's relative in the income distribution may remain unchanged. The new car, bigger house, or fancier vacation only makes one feel better off if others do not have them as well. But when economic growth lifts all boats, the positional advantage disappears. As Binswanger (2006) explains, the "positional treadmill" means that much of the extra income is spent on positional goods - items whose value depends on their relative to others - which yields no net gain in aggregate happiness [5]. The result is a zero-sum game: individual income matters a great deal for how happy one is relative to others within a country at a given point in time (a robust micro-level finding), but average income growth across an entire society does not raise average happiness (the paradoxical macro-level finding) [4].

When a country is already rich, further growth primarily shifts the of reference upward without changing relative standings, and therefore fails to produce gains in well-being [1], [4].

3. The Distinction Between Life Evaluation and Emotional Well-Being

A more nuanced argument comes from the work of Kahneman and Deaton (2010), who famously between two components of subjective well-being: life evaluation (the reflective judgment one makes about one's life overall) and emotional well-being (the day-to-day experience of positive and negative emotions). Their analysis of over 450,000 responses from the Gallup-Healthways Well-Being Index found that while life evaluation rises steadily with income (even at high levels), emotional well-being at an annual income of approximately $75,000 [6]. Beyond this , further income increases were not associated with greater daily happiness, enjoyment, or reduced stress and sadness.

This finding is echoed in the "focusing illusion" argument by Kahneman, Krueger, Schkade, Schwarz, and Stone (2006): people the contribution of income to happiness because when asked to evaluate their life, they focus on income and conventional achievements, but in moment-to-moment experience, richer people are "barely happier than others" [7]. For wealthy countries, this implies that further economic growth may improve citizens' cognitive evaluations of how well their lives are going (life satisfaction scores), but does little to improve their actual daily emotional experience.

4. Diminishing Marginal Utility of Income and Satiation of Basic Needs

A classic economic argument invokes the marginal utility of income. In poor countries, income growth enables people to meet basic needs - food, shelter, clean water, basic healthcare - and these improvements have large, demonstrable effects on happiness. However, once a country reaches a certain level of (roughly where basic needs are universally met), additional income is spent on goods and services that have much smaller marginal effects on well-being [8].

The satiation thesis rests on a plausible psychological : once a person's material needs are comfortably met, further consumption yields progressively smaller of happiness. At the national level, this translates into a flattening of the happiness-income curve for wealthy countries, even if no strict threshold exists.

5. Rising Aspirations and the Aspiration Treadmill

A related but distinct argument involves the of aspirations. As a country grows richer, its citizens' aspirations rise correspondingly. What defines a "good life" shifts upward over time. Binswanger (2006) describes this as a broader class of "treadmill effects" beyond the hedonic and positional ones, including the multi-option treadmill - where people in wealthy societies face an overwhelming number of choices, leading to increased stress, regret, and decision - and the time-saving treadmill, where labour-saving innovations intended to free up time paradoxically lead to a faster pace of life and greater time pressure [5].

The aspirational treadmill means that the goalposts for what constitutes a satisfactory of living keep moving. As Ng and Diener (2014) found, financial satisfaction remains a significant of life evaluation even in rich nations, but the very definition of what is "enough" money shifts upward as national wealth grows, preventing sustained gains in happiness [10].

6. The Role of Income Inequality

A further argument is that economic growth in wealthy countries often with rising income inequality, which undermines the happiness benefits of growth. Oishi and Kesebir (2015) demonstrated across 34 countries that "economic growth was not associated with increases in happiness when it was accompanied by growing income " [2]. They argued that earlier instances of the Easterlin paradox "can thus be explained by the frequent of economic growth and growing income inequality" [2]. Even when average income rises, if the gains accrue disproportionately to the top, the majority of citizens may feel relatively worse off (a manifestation of both the positional treadmill and the negative effects of perceived unfairness). Moreover, high inequality erodes social trust and community cohesion, which are powerful of well-being.

7. Shifting Priorities: Postmaterialist Values

As societies become wealthier and basic material security becomes widespread, citizens shift toward values - prioritizing autonomy, meaningful work, social relationships, community belonging, self-expression, and environmental quality over further material . This implies that economic growth beyond a certain point addresses needs that are increasingly less to people's happiness. In rich countries, factors such as respect, social support, and autonomy become stronger predictors of positive emotions than income does [10]. The diminishing returns to additional income are not simply a matter of adaptation, but reflect a genuine shift in what people value.

8. Opportunity Costs: Time, Relationships, and Health

Finally, there is a powerful argument about costs. The pursuit of further economic growth in wealthy countries often comes at the expense of leisure time, family relationships, community engagement, sleep, and health - all of which are robustly linked to happiness. Kahneman et al. (2006) found that people with above-average income "tend to be more tense, and do not spend more time in particularly activities" [7]. The very pursuit of higher income, driven by social comparison and the focusing illusion, may lead people to allocate excessive time to pecuniary goals while shortchanging ends such as family life and health, thereby reducing their happiness [1].

Arguments Against the Idea That Additional Wealth Does Not Increase Happiness in Rich Countries

Why Additional Wealth Can Still Increase Happiness in Rich Countries: Arguments Against the Satiation Thesis

The idea that once a country reaches a certain level of wealth, further economic growth ceases to increase the happiness of its citizens is commonly associated with the "Easterlin Paradox" - the claim that average happiness does not rise with average income over time in developed nations [1], [2]. While this view has been , there are compelling arguments against it. Below, I present a series of reasoning-based arguments that the notion that additional economic wealth makes no difference to well-being in already-rich countries.

1. The "No Satiation Point" Argument

A foundational argument against the satiation thesis is that there is no credible evidence for a specific income beyond which happiness stops rising. Stevenson and Wolfers, in a comprehensive of multiple datasets spanning many decades, found "no evidence of a satiation point beyond which wealthier countries have no further increases in subjective well-being" [1, p. 3]. They demonstrated a clear, positive log-linear between GDP per capita and average life satisfaction across countries, including among the wealthiest nations [1]. In a subsequent study specifically testing for a satiation point, they concluded that "the data show virtually no evidence of a satiation point" [9, p. 598]. This suggests that the relationship between income and well-being does not flatten out at high income levels, but continues .

2. The Compositional Argument: Different Kinds of Growth Yield Different Kinds of Happiness

It is a mistake to treat "economic growth" as a phenomenon. Growth can take different forms: it can be accompanied by rising income inequality or by more distribution; it can be environmentally destructive or sustainable; it can fund public goods or private consumption. Oishi and Kesebir showed that economic growth was not associated with increases in happiness precisely when it was accompanied by growing income inequality [7]. In countries where growth was more evenly , happiness did rise [7]. The implication is that the failure to find happiness gains from growth may be attributable to how growth occurs, not to growth itself.

3. The Argument from the Evens-Out Effect: Growth Reduces Suffering

Clark, Flèche, and Senik argued that economic growth in developed countries has a previously benefit: it reduces the variance of happiness [8]. Using data from six surveys, they found that happiness has fallen in countries that experienced income growth (but not in those that did not). Modern growth has reduced the share of both the "very unhappy" and the "perfectly happy," pulling people up from the bottom of the well-being distribution through mechanisms such as the of public goods [8]. Even if average happiness were to remain flat (which they question), the reduction of misery among the least well-off is itself a major welfare gain - and one that requires continued wealth.

4. The Aspiration Argument: Growth Raises Standards, But Also the Means to Meet Them

The classic Easterlin explanation held that growth raises aspirations, which offset the benefits of higher income, creating a "hedonic treadmill" [2]. However, this logic has a aspect: if aspirations rise with income, then stagnation would also leave citizens unhappy, because their aspirations would still be rising (driven by media, global comparisons, etc.) while the means to meet them would not. Growth is required not to make people maximally happy, but to keep with rising aspirations that are a socially-driven phenomenon. Furthermore, Veenhoven and Hagerty demonstrated that, looking at long-run data from 1946–2004, average happiness did increase slightly in rich nations and considerably in poor ones, the pure treadmill hypothesis [5].

5. The Argument from Human Development and Capabilities

Wealth is not just about direct utility from consumption - it is also about the of capabilities and freedoms. As Deaton noted, following Sen's capability approach, well-being depends on a range of functionings - health, education, security, political rights - that enable people to lead a good life [11]. These capabilities require material resources to and maintain. A richer country can afford better healthcare systems, longer and more generous education, infrastructure that reduces pollution, scientific research that extends , and social safety nets that provide security.

6. The Argument from the Security Buffer

Senik also pointed out that wealth provides a buffer stock against negative [6]. At the individual level, wealth insulates against income shocks, unemployment, health crises, and natural disasters. At the national level, a richer economy is better equipped to handle recessions, pandemics, climate change adaptation, and volatility without catastrophic welfare losses. The psychological value of security - knowing that one has resources to fall back on - is a distinct of well-being that is easily overlooked in simple happiness averages.

7. The Argument Against the Focusing Illusion

Kahneman et al. argued that people overestimate the impact of income on happiness due to a "focusing illusion" - when asked about money and happiness, people focus on income [12]. However, this argument cuts both ways. If people systematically the importance of other factors (health, social relationships, autonomy) that are also improved by economic prosperity (better healthcare, more leisure time, fewer working hours), then the true contribution of wealth to well-being may be larger than self-reports suggest. Moreover, the finding that "moment-to-moment" happiness is less with income than "life satisfaction" [12] may simply mean that money affects different dimensions of well-being.

8. The Argument from the Top of the Distribution

Even if there were returns to the average citizen, growth can still significantly improve the lives of those at the bottom of the income distribution within wealthy countries. Powdthavee, Burkhauser, and De Neve found, using the Gallup World Poll, that top incomes are associated with higher well-being, but the more important point is that even in rich countries, a minority of the population does not have their basic needs fully met [13]. Economic growth generates the tax revenues and employment opportunities that lift these individuals. To say "growth doesn't increase happiness" because the average is flat the fact that it can dramatically improve the lot of those who are worst off.

9. The Argument from Cross-Country Comparisons

If additional wealth in rich countries did not matter, we would expect to see no between GDP and happiness among high-income nations. However, Sacks, Stevenson, and Wolfers reported that the cross-country between income and life satisfaction is positive and significant even among OECD countries [3]. They found that "well-being rises with income, whether we compare people in a single country and year, whether we look across countries, or whether we look at economic growth for a given country" [3, p. 1181]. This consistent relationship across multiple levels of analysis is strong evidence against the view.

10. The Dynamic Argument: Growth and Time Series

Veenhoven and Vergunst analyzed 199 time-series from 67 nations and found a positive between GDP growth and rising happiness [10]. On average, a 1% growth in income per capita per year was followed by a rise in average happiness [10]. While the effect size was modest, it was robust and . This directly the claim that growth does not improve well-being in the long run. If it were true that wealthy countries were already at a ceiling, we would expect zero correlation - but we observe positive correlations.

Summary

The claim that additional economic wealth does not increase happiness in already-rich countries rests on a selective reading of the evidence and several logical . It conflates different types of growth, ignores effects, overlooks the expansion of capabilities and security that wealth enables, and fails to acknowledge that the data often show positive even among the wealthiest nations. The more accurate picture is that growth can and does improve well-being in rich countries - but how growth happens (equitably, sustainably, funding public goods) matters greatly for whether those gains are realized.
