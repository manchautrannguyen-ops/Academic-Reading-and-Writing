Conclusion

The claim that many customs and traditional behaviors are no longer relevant to modern life and not worth keeping contains a kernel of truth but ultimately presents an inadequate framework for understanding cultural change. Some traditions — particularly those that cause demonstrable harm or violate fundamental human rights — may indeed deserve to be left behind. However, to frame tradition as uniformly obsolete is to misunderstand both the nature of tradition (which is dynamic, not static) and the functions it serves (which address enduring human needs for identity, meaning, belonging, and social coordination).

A more sophisticated approach recognizes that the challenge of modern life is not to choose between tradition and modernity, but to engage in what Gusfield [1] identified as the complex work of navigating their interrelationship. Traditions worth keeping are those that can be reinterpreted and adapted to address contemporary challenges while continuing to provide the psychological and social benefits they have always offered. Traditions worth discarding are those that cause genuine harm, regardless of their antiquity. The final determination, however, must be made not by abstract theorizing but through democratic deliberation within communities that have the deepest stake in their own cultural heritage.
